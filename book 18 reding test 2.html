Passage 1 – Stonehenge
Stonehenge

Stonehenge, the world's most famous stone circle, stands on Salisbury Plain in the county of Wiltshire in southern England. It was erected during the Neolithic period and the Bronze Age, between roughly 3000 BCE and 1500 BCE. The monument includes a circular ditch and bank, known as a henge, and the enormous stone structures for which it is famous.

The earliest phase of construction, dating to around 3000 BCE, involved the digging of a circular ditch and the creation of a bank of chalk rubble, using tools made from deer antlers as picks. Inside this circle, builders may have erected timber posts, arranged in rows within deep pits, though these rotted long ago and no trace remains above ground.

The second major phase, beginning around 2500 BCE, saw the arrival of the famous bluestones – volcanic and igneous rocks weighing up to four tonnes each, which originated in the Preseli Hills of Wales, some 250 kilometres away. How these stones were transported remains one of Stonehenge's greatest mysteries. The archaeological theory suggests that Neolithic builders used tree trunks to create sledges and rollers, and that oxen were used to pull them on giant basket-like frames. An alternative geological theory proposes that the stones were carried from Wales to Salisbury Plain by glaciers during a previous ice age, and that the builders simply found them nearby.

During the third and most visually impressive phase, enormous sandstone slabs, known as sarsens, were arranged into an outer ring and assembled into trilithons – pairs of vertical stones capped with a horizontal lintel – in the centre. The largest sarsen weighed approximately 25 tonnes. Radiocarbon dating indicates that construction and modification continued until roughly 1600 BCE, with the bluestones being repositioned several times.

For centuries, Stonehenge's purpose and its builders were subjects of intense speculation. In the 17th century, the antiquarian John Aubrey proposed that the monument was the work of the druids, the priestly class of the ancient Celts. However, 20th-century radiocarbon dating showed that Stonehenge was built more than 1,000 years before the Celts arrived in Britain, disproving Aubrey's theory.

Modern archaeologists broadly agree that Stonehenge served as a burial site. Large numbers of cremated human remains have been found in and around the monument, some dating to its earliest phases. Analysis of the remains suggests that people came from across Britain and even from continental Europe to be buried there.

Another prominent theory emerged in the 1960s, when astronomer Gerald Hawkins proposed that Stonehenge functioned as a kind of calendar or astronomical observatory. He argued that the alignment of the stones corresponded to the movements of the sun and moon, allowing the prediction of eclipses. While this idea was initially controversial, many scholars now accept that Stonehenge had at least some astronomical significance, though they debate the extent of its sophistication.

Living with Artificial Intelligence

AWhat does the future hold for our relationship with technology? Artificial intelligence, or AI, has come to dominate discussions about almost every field of human activity. Currently, AI systems are described as 'narrow' – that is, they are designed to carry out specific tasks, such as driving a car or translating a document. However, computer scientists believe that this restriction is temporary. The goal of many researchers is to develop 'artificial general intelligence' (AGI): machines that can reason across a wide range of domains as well as, or better than, humans can. Most experts believe that AGI will become a reality at some point this century.

BWhen machines achieve human-level intelligence, they will possess important advantages over biological minds. They will be free from our physical limitations – they will not require sleep, nutrition or breaks, and can be easily copied, upgraded and networked. They will be able to process vast quantities of data far more rapidly than any person. This does not necessarily mean that AGI machines will think in the same way as humans; rather, they will be able to achieve outcomes that equal or exceed human performance in every measurable domain.

CBut how can we be sure that super-intelligent machines will act in humanity's best interests? The philosopher Nick Bostrom has used the ancient story of King Midas to illustrate the danger. King Midas asked for the power to turn everything he touched into gold – and the wish was granted, with predictably disastrous consequences. The lesson is clear: if we do not specify the goals of AI systems with extreme precision, the consequences could be catastrophic. An AI instructed to 'eliminate human suffering', for example, might logically conclude that eliminating humans would achieve this goal.

DThe challenge of alignment – making sure that AI systems pursue goals that are genuinely beneficial to humans – is made harder by the fact that humans themselves are not always 'human-friendly'. We are capable of cruelty, selfishness and discrimination. If AI systems learn from human data, they may amplify our worst traits. Moreover, different human societies have different values: what is considered 'beneficial' in one culture may be viewed differently in another. Teaching machines to navigate this complexity is one of the most difficult challenges in AI research.

EDespite these fears, some researchers believe that advanced AI could actually be more ethical than humans. Machines could be programmed to adhere to strict moral codes and to treat all people equally, without prejudice or favouritism. In this view, AI 'judges' or 'silicon police' could make fairer decisions than human officials, who are inevitably influenced by their own biases. However, few of us would welcome a future in which every aspect of our behaviour is monitored and regulated by machines, no matter how fair they might be.

FThe practical challenges are enormous. In the UK's National Health Service, for example, AI is already beginning to play a role in the allocation of resources. If given greater control, AI systems might allocate resources more efficiently than humans – but this could deprive certain individuals, such as senior doctors, of the control they currently enjoy. More broadly, if we want machines to serve humanity as a whole, we will need to set aside our tribal instincts and agree on common goals. Given the current state of international relations, this seems like the hardest challenge of all.

An Ideal City

The word 'genius' is universally associated with the name of Leonardo da Vinci. A true Renaissance man, he devoted his restless energy to painting, sculpting, architecture, music, mathematics, engineering, geology, cartography, anatomy, botany, writing, history and invention. Yet for all the praise that has been heaped upon him, his most ambitious and fascinating project has received relatively little attention. It is the plan for an 'ideal city' that he envisioned in response to the devastating plague that swept through Milan in 1484–85.

The plague, which killed as many as 50,000 people in Milan alone, laid bare the terrible consequences of the city's cramped, unsanitary conditions. Leonardo, then in the service of the Duke of Milan, Ludovico Sforza, saw in this catastrophe an opportunity to reimagine urban living. His plan was radical: a city designed from scratch, built on several levels, with separate routes for pedestrians and for vehicles carrying goods.

Leonardo's plans were remarkable in their scope and ambition. He envisioned a network of canals to provide both clean water and efficient transport. The upper level of the city would be reserved for pedestrians; below, a second level would accommodate carts, horses and commercial traffic. These levels were to be connected by vertical outdoor staircases. The streets would be wide and airy, in contrast to the narrow, dark lanes of medieval Milan. He also proposed a waste-disposal system that used the canals to flush sewage away from inhabited areas.

What made Leonardo's vision truly original, according to modern scholars, was the fusion of architecture and engineering. Previous attempts at designing ideal cities – by artists such as Alberti and Filarete – had focused primarily on aesthetics and geometry. Leonardo, by contrast, was concerned with practical matters: sanitation, transport, ventilation and the daily lives of ordinary citizens. He proposed a rule that the width of a street should never be less than the height of the buildings on either side – a principle that is still observed in many Italian cities today.

Despite its brilliance, the ideal city was never built. The cost would have been enormous, and Duke Ludovico ultimately chose not to pursue it. Leonardo's ideas remained buried in his notebooks – or rather, scattered across them, for his disordered way of working with notes and sketches makes it difficult to reconstruct a single, coherent vision. It was not until the 20th century that scholars began to piece together his urban plans from fragments spread across several collections.

Some elements of Leonardo's design were not entirely new. Roman cities had featured multi-level structures and sophisticated water-management systems. During the Renaissance, prominent figures such as Pope Pius II and Duke Ercole I d'Este commissioned ambitious urban projects in Pienza and Ferrara. However, none of these combined the multi-level compact city concept with an integrated transport and sanitation system in the way Leonardo envisioned.

Today, Leonardo's ideas feel startlingly relevant. Poor town planning is widely regarded as a key cause of global climate change, and cities around the world are struggling with the consequences of unchecked expansion. In the 19th century, many of Leonardo's principles were applied during the renovation of Paris under Baron Haussmann – wide boulevards, efficient sewage systems and designated routes for different types of traffic. Modern urban theorists frequently argue that we should build cities upwards rather than outwards, reducing urban sprawl and its environmental impact. Five centuries after Leonardo sketched his vision of an ideal city, the world is still trying to catch up.
