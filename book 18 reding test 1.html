Passage 1 – Urban Farming
Urban Farming

In Paris, urban farmers are trying a new approach to agriculture that uses no soil and very little space. Could it help cities to become self-sufficient in food?

On top of a huge exhibition hall in southern Paris, the world's largest urban rooftop farm has started to bear fruit. Strawberries that are small, tasty and growing demands have been occasionally picked here since May 2020, and a handful of other crops are pushing up, green, from large, white, planting bags. Pascal Hardy, an engineer and consultant in sustainable development, founded this company, Agripolis, in 2018. The farm, which measures 14,000 square metres and is on top of Paris Expo, a convention centre in the 15th arrondissement, aims to grow more than 1,000 kg of fruit and vegetables in high season, in over 20 varieties including lettuces, strawberries, herbs, aubergines and tomatoes.

This is far from the sum of Hardy's ambitions. He plans to build a vast vertical farm in the south of the city – essentially a column of plants, standing almost like an apartment block – that could eventually provide 10% of Paris's total food consumption. Meanwhile, other schemes to produce food in cities are being developed in Shanghai, Singapore, New York and Tokyo, in countries where the cost of land is high and the distances food currently has to travel to reach consumers are bytes.

In many cities, growing crops on vacant lots has a long history – allotments in the UK go back at least to the 19th century – and has recently been given fresh impetus by movements such as transition towns and guerrilla gardening. But new technologies are now changing the picture. Aeroponic farming, for example, grows plants in an air mist rather than in the ground or water. In this system, plants are grown in vertical tubes, which not only requires minimal space but also minimises water use by up to 90%. Advocates say this also eliminates the need for pesticides, as there is no soil for pests to breed in, and reduces transportation journeys, as food can be grown close to where it is consumed.

Hardy is not the only entrepreneur in this market. In the USA, there are a number of established vertical and indoor farming operations, including Plenty, Bowery Farming and AeroFarms. AeroFarms, which operates one of the world's largest indoor vertical farms in Newark, New Jersey, uses aeroponic systems to grow leafy greens. Such systems, say proponents, give producers more control over growing conditions, and allow them to choose crop varieties based on flavour rather than ability to withstand transportation.

For now, commercial urban farming is relatively small-scale. But advocates say that aeroponic urban farms could be run above or below ground. Underground car parks, for example, could be transformed into farms, as could the basements of large buildings. The technology uses a tiny fraction of the electricity of conventional greenhouses, thanks to LED lighting that allows farmers to optimise wavelengths for plant growth. The cost of the produce is comparable to that of soil-based organic produce, and cheaper than hydroponic alternatives.

There are some things, however, that aeroponic urban farms are not suited to growing. Root vegetables, for instance, need soil. And crops such as wheat and rice require much larger areas than city farms can provide. Some crops, such as beans, are better suited to summer months. But for salad leaves, herbs, strawberries and tomatoes, the method works well. Research at various universities suggests that the nutritional value of aeroponically grown produce is at least equal to that of soil-grown equivalents, and may in some cases be higher.

Forest management in Pennsylvania, USA

AHow does a tree's 'value' change when it is used for different purposes? That depends on the species of tree, its size, its condition and the landowner's goals. Some trees are valuable as timber – as 'ichiban' (number one) grade logs, used for high-quality furniture. Others provide wildlife habitat: food and shelter for birds and mammals. A tree might be worth very little as a log but have great ecological importance. In Pennsylvania, where about 60% of the state is forested, the decisions that forest landowners make affect millions of hectares and have a major impact on environmental quality, biodiversity and the state's economy.

BHistorically, Pennsylvania's forests have been exploited for their most commercially valuable trees through a practice known as 'high-grading'. In this type of cutting, only the biggest, healthiest, most commercially desirable trees were removed, leaving the forest populated by poor-quality trees, sometimes called 'junk trees'. High-grading has negative long-term consequences for the forest: the genetic quality of the remaining trees tends to be lower, the structure of the forest is degraded, and the overall health of the ecosystem declines. Essentially, by always taking the best and leaving the worst, high-grading leads to forests that are progressively less productive and less valuable over time – both economically and ecologically.

CThere is, however, an alternative approach that can make harvesting low-quality trees economically viable while also benefiting the forest. Wood energy markets provide a use for trees that have little or no value as timber. These markets use low-quality wood for generating heat and electricity through combustion, or for producing liquid fuels for vehicles. In Pennsylvania and neighbouring states, several biomass energy plants are in operation, and more are planned. They create a market for wood that was previously considered waste. This gives forest landowners an economic incentive to remove poor-quality trees, which can improve the overall health and quality of the remaining forest.

DOne important method for improving forest quality is the Timber Stand Improvement (TSI) cut. TSI cuts target trees that are competing with more desirable species for sunlight, water and nutrients. By removing these competitors – which often include invasive plants and non-native species – the remaining trees are freed to grow faster and healthier. TSI cuts do not aim to generate significant revenue; their primary purpose is ecological improvement. However, if a nearby wood energy market exists, the wood removed in TSI cuts can at least be sold for some return, rather than simply being left to rot or burned on site.

ESalvage cuts serve a different but equally important purpose. These operations target trees that are dead, dying or damaged – often the result of insect infestations, diseases or storm damage. In Pennsylvania, the emerald ash borer and hemlock woolly adelgid are two examples of insects that have devastated large areas of forest. Salvage cuts can help to slow the spread of such pests by removing infected trees before the insects can move to healthy neighbours. The wood from salvage cuts may have limited timber value, but it can often be used in wood energy markets. By enabling the removal of trees that would otherwise become sources of further fire risk and pest infestation, salvage operations contribute to overall forest health.

FA third type of cutting, the shelterwood cut, is used to regenerate the forest. In this method, mature trees are gradually removed over a period of years, allowing young seedlings to become established under the partial shade of the remaining canopy. Once the new generation of trees is firmly rooted, the last of the older trees are removed. This approach allows foresters to control which species regenerate – for example, by promoting oak or cherry over less desirable species. The shelterwood method is particularly useful in forests that have been degraded by decades of high-grading, as it allows landowners to essentially 'reset' the species composition.

GWhile it is important to harvest low-quality wood for energy and forest improvement, it is equally important to recognise that some 'low-use' wood should be retained. Dead standing trees (snags) and fallen logs provide essential nutrients to the forest floor as they decompose. Trees with cavities – holes created by decay or woodpeckers – offer nesting sites and shelter for a wide range of wildlife, from owls and wood ducks to squirrels and bats. Certain small trees and shrubs, such as hawthorn and dogwood, produce berries and fruits that are critical food sources for birds and mammals. These elements are part of the 'structural diversity' that makes a forest ecosystem healthy and resilient. Species that are rare in a given area should also be left to grow, as they add to the genetic diversity of the forest. A responsible approach to forest management balances the economic benefits of wood removal with the ecological need to maintain a complex, diverse and functioning forest ecosystem.

Conquering Earth's space junk problem

ALast year, commercial companies, military and civil departments and amateurs sent more than 400 satellites into orbit, over four times the yearly average in the previous decade. Numbers could rise even more sharply if leading space companies follow through on plans to deploy hundreds to thousands of large constellations of satellites to space in the next few years. All that traffic can lead to disaster. Ten years ago, a US commercial Iridium satellite smashed into an inactive Russian communications satellite called Cosmos-2251, creating thousands of new pieces of space shrapnel that now threaten other satellites in low Earth orbit – the zone stretching up to 2,000 kilometres in altitude. Altogether, there are roughly 20,000 human-made objects in orbit, from working satellites to small rocket pieces. And satellite operators can't steer away from every potential crash, because each move consumes time and fuel that could otherwise be used for the spacecraft's main job.

BConcern about space junk goes back to the beginning of the satellite era, but the number of objects in orbit is rising so rapidly that researchers are investigating new ways of attacking the problem. Several government agencies and companies are developing or testing 'space cleanup' technologies. One broad approach involves tracking debris more precisely, so that operators can steer their craft away more efficiently. Another approach is to build satellites to be less wasteful – for instance, by designing them for controlled reentry so that they burn up in the atmosphere at the end of their lives, rather than becoming permanent orbital junk. A third, more radical approach is 'active debris removal': physically grabbing or deflecting pieces of space junk.

CThe challenge starts with tracking. Although the US military's Space Surveillance Network tracks some 20,000 pieces of debris larger than 10 cm, there are an estimated 500,000 pieces between 1 and 10 cm and more than 100 million smaller than 1 cm. The tiniest fragments are essentially invisible, yet even a fleck of paint can cause significant damage at orbital speeds. Improving tracking technology is essential, and several nations are investing in upgraded ground-based radar and optical systems, as well as sensors in space itself.

DOne innovative idea for debris removal comes from the European Space Agency (ESA), which has proposed a mission called ClearSpace-1, planned for 2025. The mission would use a 'chaser' spacecraft with four robotic arms to capture a piece of debris – specifically, a payload adapter left over from a 2013 Vega rocket launch – and drag it into Earth's atmosphere to burn up. Other proposals include using lasers to slow down debris and cause it to re-enter the atmosphere, deploying nets or harpoons to snag fragments, and using magnets on spacecraft to attract and de-orbit metallic junk.

ESome experts worry about the legal and political complications of space cleanup. International law currently holds that a nation retains ownership of its space objects indefinitely, even if they are no longer functional. This means that one country cannot simply remove another's defunct satellite without permission, even if it poses a collision risk. Resolving these jurisdictional issues will require new international agreements. Another concern is the potential for cleanup technology to be misused. A spacecraft capable of grabbing defunct satellites could also, in theory, be used to interfere with operational ones – raising fears about the weaponisation of space. In addition, the sheer cost of active debris removal makes it crucial to develop sustainability practices that prevent the problem from getting worse in the first place.

FSpace sustainability involves several principles. First, new satellites should be designed for disposal at the end of their lives – either through controlled de-orbiting or by moving to a 'graveyard orbit' far from active spacecraft. Second, launch vehicles should be designed to minimise the debris they produce; rocket stages should be de-orbited, and measures should be taken to prevent explosions of old rocket bodies, which are a major source of new fragments. Third, operators of large satellite constellations – such as SpaceX's Starlink network, which could eventually number more than 40,000 satellites – must be held to high standards of debris mitigation. If even a small fraction of those satellites fail to de-orbit properly, they could significantly worsen the debris problem. Some analysts have proposed requiring satellite operators to post a 'space bond' or take out insurance, so that if a company goes bankrupt and can't de-orbit its fleet, funds are available for cleanup.

GThe stakes are high. If the amount of debris in orbit reaches a tipping point – a scenario known as 'Kessler syndrome', after NASA scientist Donald Kessler, who described it in 1978 – collisions could become self-sustaining, with each crash producing new fragments that cause further collisions in a chain reaction. This could eventually make certain orbits unusable, with devastating consequences for communications, weather monitoring, navigation and scientific research. Preventing this scenario will require a combination of better tracking, international cooperation, new technology, responsible satellite design and, almost certainly, active debris removal on a much larger scale than anything yet attempted.
