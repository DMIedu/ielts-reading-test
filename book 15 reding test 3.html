Passage 1 – Henry Moore
Henry Moore
Henry Moore (1898–1986) was one of the most celebrated sculptors of the twentieth century. His monumental organic forms can be found in public spaces around the world.

AHenry Spencer Moore was born in Castleford, Yorkshire, England, the seventh of eight children of a coal miner. From an early age he was determined to become a sculptor, inspired in part by stories of Michelangelo told to him by his Sunday school teacher. Despite his father’s wish that he train as a teacher, Moore won a scholarship to the Leeds School of Art in 1919, then gained a place at the Royal College of Art in London. He later acknowledged that his training there had been less influential than his regular visits to the British Museum, where he was captivated by the pre-Columbian, Egyptian and African sculpture in the collection. These ancient works had a directness and power he found lacking in the European classical tradition.

BIn the 1920s, Moore began experimenting with direct carving – cutting directly into the stone or wood, rather than modelling in clay and having the work cast or carved by assistants. He felt this more honest approach revealed the true nature of the material. He also drew extensively, using drawing as a way of generating ideas that he would later translate into three dimensions. His sketchbooks are filled with studies of bones, pebbles, shells and natural forms, reflecting his conviction that sculpture should be rooted in the organic world. ‘Sculpture is an art of the open air,’ he said. ‘Daylight, sunlight, is necessary to it.’

CMoore was fascinated by the relationship between solid form and space. He began introducing holes into his sculptures – a radical innovation at the time – arguing that a hole could have as much energy and presence as a solid mass. ‘A hole can have as much shape-meaning as a solid mass,’ he wrote. This preoccupation led to some of his most iconic works: reclining figures with limbs that arch away from the body, creating enclosed and open spaces simultaneously.

DMoore was also innovative in his choice of materials. Where classical sculptors had favoured marble, Moore worked in a wide range of stone, wood, lead, bronze and concrete. He argued that each material had its own character, and that the sculptor’s task was to understand and work with that character rather than against it. Bronze became his preferred medium for large public works from the 1950s onwards, partly because of its durability and partly because it could be produced in editions, making his work available to more institutions.

EThe size of Moore’s output grew dramatically after the Second World War. He produced increasingly monumental works for public spaces: outside the UNESCO headquarters in Paris, in front of the Lincoln Center in New York, in the grounds of the Tate Gallery in London. He believed sculpture should be encountered in open public spaces, where it could interact with the landscape and sky, rather than confined to museum interiors. ‘The world needs more large sculpture,’ he declared, ‘and open spaces are crying out for it.’

FOne of the most distinctive features of Moore’s mature work is the use of holes and voids as structural and expressive elements. Critics who initially dismissed this as mere trickery came to recognise it as a profound engagement with the nature of three-dimensional form. Henry Read, the art critic, described Moore as the greatest sculptor of the twentieth century, arguing that his work had reconnected sculpture with its prehistoric roots while remaining thoroughly contemporary.

GIn later life, Moore’s studio became a foundation which managed the reproduction of his work and the maintenance of his estate in Hertfordshire. The Henry Moore Foundation continues to support sculpture and related art forms. Despite the enormous prices his works now command at auction, Moore remained throughout his life a remarkably modest and practical man, more interested in the making of things than in the art world’s attention to his landscape of fame.

The return of the huarango
In the desert valleys of southern Peru, a campaign to replant an ancient tree is helping restore a damaged ecosystem and the livelihoods of local communities.

AThe huarango tree (Prosopis pallida) is a remarkable plant. It can live for thousands of years, fix nitrogen into the soil, and survive in conditions of extreme drought by extending its roots up to 50 metres into the ground to reach water. For millennia it was the anchor of ecosystems in the arid river valleys of coastal Peru, providing food, fuel and timber for human communities, shade and leaf litter to enrich the soil, and habitat for wildlife. Yet over the past five centuries – and particularly in the twentieth century – the huarango has been brought close to extinction in its native habitat. Today barely 1 per cent of the original huarango forests remain.

BThe story of the huarango’s decline is in many ways the story of the decline of the civilisation that depended on it. Archaeologists believe the ancient Nasca people, famous for the mysterious lines they carved into the desert plateau, cleared much of the huarango forest for agriculture. When the trees went, the roots that held the soil in place went with them. A series of El Niño floods then washed away the topsoil, turning once-fertile valleys into the barren desert that exists today. Some scholars believe the collapse of Nasca civilisation around 600 AD was directly linked to this environmental catastrophe.

CToday a coalition of scientists, local communities and international organisations is working to reverse this damage through a large-scale replanting programme. The project is based at Cahuachi, a site in the Nasca valley that was once a major ceremonial centre of the Nasca people. Led by archaeologist Giuseppe Orefici, the team has planted over a million huarango trees since the project began. The work is painstaking: each sapling must be watered by hand during its first year, and many do not survive the fierce desert heat.

DThe return of the huarango is already having measurable ecological effects. Soil quality in replanted areas has improved significantly, and the trees are beginning to provide food and habitat for native birds and insects. Local farmers report that fields near huarango plantations retain moisture better and are more productive. In one valley, a spring that had been dry for decades began to flow again after huarango roots penetrated to the water table.

EBeyond ecology, the project has significant social dimensions. Replanting the huarango has become a source of community identity and pride. Local schools incorporate the history and ecology of the tree into their curricula. Farmers who take part in the planting programme receive training in sustainable agriculture and access to new markets for huarango-derived products, including pods used as animal feed, flour, and syrup. These economic benefits are important in an area where poverty is widespread.

FScientists involved in the project are also exploring the wider potential of the huarango as a tool for ecological restoration in other arid regions. Its ability to fix nitrogen, tolerate extreme drought and create conditions that allow other plants to establish makes it what ecologists call a ‘nurse tree’: one that creates the conditions in which other species can return. Similar restoration projects using related Prosopis species are now underway in parts of Africa and the Middle East.

Why we need to protect polar bears
Polar bears are facing multiple threats from climate change to pollution. But their value to science – and to us – goes far beyond their iconic status.

AThe polar bear (Ursus maritimus) has become the defining image of climate change: a solitary animal standing on a shrinking island of ice, marooned in a warming sea. This powerful image has made the polar bear one of the most recognisable symbols of the environmental movement. But campaigners warn that iconic status alone is not enough to save it. The threats to polar bears are multiple, serious and accelerating, and the arguments for protecting them go beyond simple sentiment.

BThe most immediate threat to polar bears is the loss of sea ice. Polar bears depend on sea ice as a platform from which to hunt seals, their primary prey. As Arctic temperatures rise at twice the global average, the ice is melting earlier in spring and forming later in autumn, dramatically reducing the time bears have to feed. A bear that cannot build up enough fat reserves during the ice season will struggle to survive the ice-free summer months. Studies in Hudson Bay show that bears are already lighter on average than they were 30 years ago, and that survival rates among cubs have fallen.

CA second major threat is hunting. Although commercial hunting of polar bears was banned by international agreement in 1973, subsistence hunting by indigenous communities remains legal in Canada, Greenland and Russia. Annual quotas are set by national governments, but conservationists worry that in some areas the quotas are not sustainable given the declining bear population. Trophy hunting – the shooting of bears by wealthy tourists – is still permitted in Canada and remains deeply controversial.

DPollution is a less visible but serious threat. Polar bears sit at the top of the Arctic food chain, and as toxic chemicals accumulate at each level of that chain, bears end up with high concentrations of pollutants in their bodies. Studies have found that bears in some populations carry levels of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and other industrial chemicals high enough to damage their immune and reproductive systems. This form of chemical pollution is particularly insidious because it has no single source and cannot be addressed by local action alone.

EThe scientific case for protecting polar bears rests partly on their role as an indicator species. Because they sit at the top of the food chain and are sensitive to changes in the availability of sea ice, polar bears serve as a kind of living barometer of the health of the Arctic ecosystem. Monitoring their condition, distribution and breeding success gives scientists a detailed picture of how the entire ecosystem is responding to climate change. In this sense, investing in polar bear research is investing in our understanding of one of the planet’s most important and vulnerable environments.

FThe economic arguments for conservation are also compelling. The polar bear tourism industry is worth hundreds of millions of dollars annually in places like Churchill, Manitoba, where every October thousands of visitors come to watch the bears gathering on the shores of Hudson Bay. The loss of polar bears would devastate local economies that depend on this ecotourism, as well as the indigenous communities for whom the bear holds deep cultural significance.

GPerhaps most importantly, polar bears have a role to play as ambassadors for the natural world in the broadest sense. Research shows that people who engage with polar bear conservation campaigns are more likely to develop a concern for environmental issues generally. Protecting polar bears from extinction therefore serves not only their interests, but also the interests of a far wider range of species and ecosystems that do not enjoy the same level of public sympathy. A world that saves the polar bear is a world that has developed the habits of mind and the political will to tackle environmental problems at scale.
