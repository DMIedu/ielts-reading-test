Passage 1 – Materials to take us beyond concrete
Materials to take us beyond concrete

What alternatives are there to this widely used but environmentally damaging building material?

AConcrete is the second most used substance on earth, after water. It is estimated that the production of cement – theite key component in concrete – is responsible for 7–8% of global carbon dioxide emissions. With the world's population growing and urbanising rapidly, demand for concrete is set to rise even further. The challenge is clear: can we house a growing population without carbon emissions soaring?

BEngineering professor Chris Cheeseman of Imperial College London notes that concrete is 'a marvellous material' – mouldable, pourable, and, when reinforced with steel, extremely strong. Steel and concrete expand at the same rate when heated, making them ideal partners. 'The problem isn't really the material itself,' says Cheeseman. 'It's the scale at which we use it.' The sheer volume of concrete manufactured globally is what makes the environmental impact so severe.

COne reason concrete is so dominant is that its ingredients – calcium, silicon, aluminium and iron – are found in abundance in most places around the globe. It is cheap, readily available, and highly versatile. No other material matches its combination of strength, flexibility, ease of use and low cost. These properties make it extraordinarily hard to replace.

DUsing wood as a construction material may sound medieval, but climate change is driving architects to turn to treated timber for even the tallest buildings. Timber towers are appearing in cities such as Vancouver, Vienna and Brumunddal in Norway, where the world's tallest wooden building, Mjøstårnet, stands at 85.4 metres. These projects demonstrate that wood can be used for large-scale construction.

EOf course, wood has drawbacks. It expands as it absorbs moisture from the air, is susceptible to pests, and can be a fire risk. But advances in engineered timber, particularly cross-laminated timber (CLT), have addressed many of these problems. An adhesive is used to stick layers of solid-sawn timber together, creating panels that are strong, light and dimensionally stable. Markus Mannstrom, an executive at Swedish timber company Södra, says that wooden buildings can be constructed at a greater speed than ones of concrete and steel, and with far less noise.

FHowever, experts caution that wood is not yet proven at scale. Only when a wider range of construction projects – including hospitals, schools and other public buildings – has been successfully completed will it be possible to see timber as a real alternative to concrete. More high-rise wooden buildings are needed before the construction industry can regard wood as a viable option.

GAnother approach is to find replacements for cement itself. Industrial waste products such as fly ash – a byproduct of coal-burning power plants – and iron-ore slag, produced during the smelting of iron ore, can partially substitute for Portland cement in concrete mixes. These materials can reduce the carbon footprint of concrete significantly. Felix Preston and Johanna Lehne of Chatham House say that new cement alternatives face resistance from the industry, partly due to the higher costs involved and the need for new supply chains.

HAnna Surgenor of the UK's Green Building Council points out that the environmental benefits of waste-product cements must be calculated across the entire life cycle of a building. Transporting waste materials over long distances using fossil-fuel-powered vehicles may negate the carbon savings. Each project must be assessed individually, she says, and blanket claims about sustainability should be treated with caution.

The steam car

The successes and failures of the Doble brothers and their steam cars

AIn the early days of the automobile, steam was a serious contender as a power source. Steam had already proved itself in railways and factories. However, the first steam cars inherited both the strengths and weaknesses of the technology. They were powerful and smooth, but they required manual boiler ignition – a process that could take up to 30 minutes – and carried only enough water for short journeys. Despite these drawbacks, steam cars found a loyal following among early motorists.

BThe rise of the internal combustion engine gradually eclipsed steam power. Early gasoline-powered cars were difficult to start, requiring a hand crank that could be dangerous, but the introduction of the electric starter by General Motors in 1912 removed this disadvantage. Gasoline cars became cheaper, easier to operate and more convenient. By the early 1920s, steam cars had virtually disappeared from American roads. Their success had been short-lived.

CInto this unpromising landscape came the Doble brothers – Abner, John, Warren and Bill. Between 1906 and 1909, the teenage brothers built their first steam car prototypes in the basement of their parents' home, using parts salvaged from wrecked cars and industrial scrap. They had no formal engineering training, relying instead on instinct, experimentation and an encyclopaedic knowledge of steam technology passed down from their father.

DBy 1917, the brothers had developed the Model B, a car that generated considerable interest for its speed, silence and almost instantaneous startup – a major breakthrough for steam technology. The positive attention attracted investors, and Abner Doble formed the General Engineering Company in Emeryville, California. Orders poured in, and the future looked bright.

EUnfortunately, early customers of the Model B experienced a number of mechanical problems. Cars were occasionally sluggish, and in some cases moved in the wrong direction when the driver attempted to go forward. These faults, combined with production delays, led to widespread negative publicity and damaged the company's reputation. Customers who had placed orders began to demand refunds.

FUndeterred, Abner Doble focused his efforts on a new model – the Model E. In 1924, the Automobile Club of America conducted a road test. The car had been left outside overnight in freezing temperatures. Within 40 seconds of the ignition being turned on, the car was ready to drive. The testers were astonished by its silence, smooth handling and acceleration. Its maximum speed was pushed to over a hundred miles per hour, while keeping its emissions extremely low. The Model E received glowing reviews and attracted positive publicity at last.

GDespite the acclaim, the Model E was never a commercial success. Each car was essentially handmade and cost as much as a Rolls-Royce. Abner Doble constantly tinkered with the design, preventing the company from achieving efficient mass production. Fewer than fifty of the amazing Model E steam cars had been produced before the company went out of business in 1931. Yet even today, several Model Es remain on the road in the United States. They are straightforward to maintain, and they run clean enough to pass the emissions laws in California today – and, as one owner puts it, 'they are pretty strict.' Perhaps, with modern technology and materials, the steam car could yet offer a possible solution to the automotive issues of today.

The case for mixed-ability classes

The practice of grouping students by academic ability, known as 'streaming' or 'tracking', is common in schools worldwide. But is it the best approach?

Consider a Year 10 English class studying Romeo and Juliet. In one corner, a gifted student analyses the play's themes with sophistication. In another, a student with dyslexia struggles to decode the text. At the back, two students have English as a second language and are working through a simplified version. The teacher moves between groups, adjusting explanations and tasks. This is a mixed-ability class in action – messy, challenging and, many educators argue, highly effective.

Streaming has obvious appeal. Teachers can pitch lessons at the right level. Students are not held back by slower peers or left behind by faster ones. Resources can be targeted more efficiently. It seems logical and tidy. Many parents and politicians favour it, and in some countries it is the norm from an early age.

However, the evidence against streaming is substantial. Professor John Hattie of the Melbourne Education Research Institute has synthesised over 300 studies on the topic. His conclusion is stark: there is very little indication that streaming leads to higher academic achievements for any group. The most significant negative impact is on those students placed in the bottom sets, especially where a large proportion of them come from disadvantaged backgrounds. Meanwhile, for the brightest pupils, there appears to be only minimal advantage. A further issue is that teachers tend to have lower expectations of students in streamed groups.

Critics of mixed-ability teaching often use the analogy of a group hike. If you put experienced hikers with beginners, the argument goes, the fast walkers will be frustrated and the slow walkers will be exhausted. Everyone would be better off in separate groups. It's the educational equivalent of Mount Qomolangma: the brightest students never reach the summit because they are weighed down by the demands of the collective.

The constructivist theory of learning, associated with the psychologist Lev Vygotsky, provides a powerful counter-argument. Vygotsky emphasised the 'zone of proximal development' (ZPD) – the gap between what a student can achieve alone and what they can achieve with support. Teaching, in this model, is 'scaffolding': providing assistance that is gradually removed as competence grows. The key insight is that the 'more knowledgeable other' (MKO) – the person providing support – does not have to be the teacher.

In a mixed-ability classroom, students frequently act as MKOs for their peers. A student who mastered fractions last week can explain them to a classmate in language a teacher might never think to use. The act of teaching reinforces the MKO's own understanding. Research consistently shows that students who teach recently acquired knowledge deepen their own learning significantly. It can be a genuinely rewarding experience.

None of this means that mixed-ability teaching is easy. It demands skilled teachers, creative lesson planning and a willingness to let go of traditional notions of classroom control. But the evidence suggests that, done well, it benefits the majority of students far more than streaming does. The goal of education should not be to polish a few bright stars while leaving the rest in the dark. It should be to ensure that as many students as possible reach their full potential.
